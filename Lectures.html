1.1.3 Structure, Context, and Distinctive Themes of the Lectures (11-26)

Hegel's God is not a supersensible entity, a "supreme being", but rather the ultimate condition of possibility for the totality of experience and for religious experience in particular, the experience of the "religious relationship" of finite and infinite. (12)

In this sense, the philosophical first principle and the theological first principle are one and the same: God = absolute idea. (12)

God is actual, "absolute actuality", "actual being in and for itself"-- but only in and through worldly reality, not as a separated, supersensible entity. (12)

Apart from the world, God's actuality remains abstract, unrealized; in and through the world, of which God is the ideal condition of possibility, God becomes concrete, living, true actuality–or absolute spirit. (12)

The logical deep structure is grounded in the dialectic of the syllogism, which is the basic movement of thought itself. (12)

Three moments (figures) of any syllogism (according to Hegel) are:

  • Universality
  • Particularity
  • Individuality/Singularity

Example Syllogism.

  • All human beings are mortal
  • Socrates is human
  • Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

Universal Substance: Human Beings

Particular Quality: Mortal

Individual Subject: Socrates
